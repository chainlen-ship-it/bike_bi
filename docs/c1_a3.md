# A3 Thinking — Cyclistic Bike-Share: Course 1

**Goal:** Ensure Cyclistic has sufficient bikes at the right station locations to meet rider demand across New York City.

---

## Why A3

A3 was chosen as the project framework coming out of the HubSpot RevOps certification. It maps cleanly onto BI problem definition and produces a document that works at every level simultaneously — the goal and solution for the executive, the analysis questions and data design for the analyst, the full decision trail for anyone who picks this up cold.

The central insight that drove every design decision: **a station with low trip counts isn't necessarily low-demand. It may simply be under-supplied.** The dashboard exists to separate those two realities before anyone makes a fleet or station investment decision.

---

## 1. Background

Cyclistic operates bike-share across NYC boroughs in partnership with the city. The Customer Growth Team is creating a business plan for next year and needs to understand how customers use the bikes — specifically where demand exists and where the fleet is failing to meet it.

Dataset spans 2013–2015 NYC Citi Bike trips plus US Census Bureau boundary data for geographic context. Three years of data was chosen deliberately: 2015 alone tells you what happened, 2013–2015 tells you whether it's a trend or an anomaly.

Primary dataset: `bigquery-public-data.new_york_citibike.citibike_trips`
Secondary dataset: `bigquery-public-data.geo_census_tracts` + `census_bureau_acs`

---

## 2. Current Conditions

- Station demand is uneven across the network but there is no visibility into whether low-activity stations represent low demand or low supply
- Bikes get misallocated over time — high-departure stations drain inventory, high-arrival stations accumulate it — but there is no systematic way to identify which stations are which
- No connection between station activity and surrounding population density, so there is no way to anticipate where demand will shift as neighborhoods grow
- The existing data has trip starts and ends but no fleet-level view — you can see where trips happen but not whether trips were suppressed because bikes weren't available
- No weather integration, so seasonal patterns are visible but weather-driven variation cannot be separated from demand-driven variation

The core problem: **a station showing low trips but sitting in a high-density census tract isn't low-demand, it's under-supplied.** Drawing the wrong conclusion from that data leads to the wrong infrastructure decisions.

---

## 3. Goals / Targets

- Classify stations into activity tiers using 33rd/66th percentile reference lines — not hard buckets, but visual guides that let the data tell its own story against a consistent framework
- Build a send/return balance matrix per station: drain (more departures than arrivals), accumulator (more arrivals than departures), balanced (self-sustaining)
- Size total fleet need: peak concurrent bikes in use plus 30% buffer plus 5% maintenance pool
- Layer Census Bureau population data to flag latent demand zones — stations where low activity and high nearby population density intersect
- Design for two stakeholder personas simultaneously: executive overview (Sara Romero, Ernest Cox) and analyst drill-down (Tessa Blackwell)

**Why percentile reference lines instead of hard tiers:**
Hard tiers create an artificial boundary that hides the real distribution. A station at the 34th percentile and one at the 32nd percentile are nearly identical operationally but would fall into different labeled buckets. Reference lines show the full distribution and let the viewer draw their own conclusions about where meaningful breaks exist.

**Why the buffer formula matters:**
Peak concurrent bikes × (1 + buffer%) = total fleet needed. The buffer slider in the dashboard makes this math visible and adjustable. Stakeholders can model scenarios — what if we run a 40% buffer to account for maintenance spikes? — without needing a new query. The math runs client-side.

---

## 4. Analysis Questions the Data Must Answer

- Which stations are high/medium/low activity by trip volume, and how has that changed 2013–2015?
- What is the send/return balance per station — which stations are draining inventory and which are accumulating it?
- How does usage vary by season, year, and rider type (subscriber vs casual customer)?
- Where does Census population density predict future demand mismatches with current station activity?
- What is peak concurrent bike usage, and what fleet size does that imply at various buffer levels?
- Which destination stations attract the most total trip minutes — indicating willingness to travel, not just proximity?

---

## 5. Proposed Countermeasures

- 11 BigQuery SQL queries producing analytical outputs and two materialized reporting tables
- HTML dashboard with live fleet calculator, interactive station map, seasonal trend chart, and population context layer
- Census overlay as a directional layer — not a precise prediction but a signal for where to look before making station investment decisions
- Dual-persona dashboard design: executive KPI strip for high-level stakeholders, analyst drill-down table for detailed review

## Dashboard Design Intent

- **Primary view:** 2015 data (most complete, post-ramp year)
- **Secondary:** 2013–2015 year-over-year trend
- **Map:** Leaflet NYC map with checkbox filters for activity tier and rider type, directional trip flow lines, companion table sorted by drain risk, station detail cards on click
- **Buffer parameter:** live slider (10–50%, default 30%) drives fleet needed calculation client-side — no re-query required
- **Population layer:** Census tract shading with latent demand zone callouts where low station activity meets high population density

Accessibility: large print and text-to-speech alternatives required per stakeholder Sara Romero.
